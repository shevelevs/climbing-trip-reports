# Trip Report: Mt Laurel via NE Gully

### Summary
 * **Date:** September 10, 2022
 * **Team:** Sergey
 * **Route:** NE Gully (Convict Lake Approach)
 * **Style:** Scramble / Day Hike
 * **Total Time:** 6h 0m
 * **Total Distance**: 10.92mi
 * **Total Elevation Gain**: 3,590ft

## 1. The Approach & Dry Metamorphic Rock
<img src="./photo_1.jpg" align="right" alt="Mt Laurel NE Gully Route Overlay" width="400">

I started my morning at the Convict Lake trailhead under crisp, clear September Sierra skies. Mt Laurel (11,812') and its neighbor Mt Morrison tower dramatically over the deep blue waters of Convict Lake, showing off their famous contorted metamorphic rock bands. 

The approach leads past the lake and up into Laurel Canyon. I followed the rugged 4WD road winding up the canyon alongside Laurel Creek. The approach went smoothly and the route was completely dry, with no lingering snow or ice in the gully. The path was straightforward and brought me directly beneath the massive northeast face of Mt Laurel.

---

## 2. Navigating the NE Gully Maze
<img src="./photo_3.jpg" align="right" alt="Looking up the steep NE Gully steps" width="320">

Leaving the road behind, I entered the mouth of the NE Gully. Rather than a standard talus or scree chute, the climb felt like a vertical canyoneering expedition through a complex maze of metamorphic rock. 

The route gets technical right from the start, with the first few hundred yards presenting the hardest scrambling on the route—a series of short 5.2 bulges. Moving unroped in sticky rubber approach shoes, the climbing felt secure and not particularly difficult. 

Higher up, the canyon opens into a geologic playground. The main path climbs a clean, pumice-like rock substrate that offers pleasant scrambling, flanked by a sea of decaying loose choss. I crossed the striking 80-foot-wide vein of red rock that sweeps across the gully, climbing gray slabs and passing through bands of red, white, and black rock. 

However, it was hard to track the exact line inside the maze of gullies, and I definitely went off route. While one is normally supposed to gain the ridge south of the summit, I ended up topping out on the ridge a little north of it. The rock quality on the ridge itself was not good—everything was rotten and required very careful steps.

---

## 3. Summit Ridge & Sandy Descent
<img src="./photo_12.jpg" align="right" alt="Approach shoe buried in sand" width="300">

I made my way along the ridge to the summit of Mt Laurel (11,812 ft). The reward was a breathtaking panoramic view of Mono Lake to the north and the contorted peaks of the Sierra.

However, the weather was changing quickly, and scary dark storm clouds rolled in over the peak. I knew I needed to get off the summit immediately, and ended up coming down just in time.

<img src="./photo_10.jpg" align="left" alt="Scary storm clouds rolling in over Mt Laurel" width="340" style="margin-right: 15px; margin-bottom: 10px;">

For the descent, I veered northward toward Convict Lake. It was a pretty unpleasant sandy "skiing" descent down the steep scree slopes. Plunging down the deep gravel was fast, but it buried my approach shoes completely in the sand. After a lot of sliding and shaking out my shoes, I negotiated some thick brush along the final trail and completed the 10.9-mile solo loop in exactly 6 hours.
